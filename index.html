Foundations

What is a Tragedy?

A tragedy is a dramatic work depicting the downfall of a noble or great character. Rooted in Ancient Greek theatre, it tells the story of a hero whose own flaws lead to their destruction.

"Tragedy is an imitation of an action that is serious, complete, and of a certain magnitude… through pity and fear effecting the proper purgation of these emotions." — Aristotle, Poetics (c. 335 BCE)

Key Elements

  • Tragic Hero — A protagonist of noble stature with a fatal flaw
  • Hamartia — The hero's tragic flaw or error in judgment
  • Peripeteia — A sudden reversal of fortune
  • Anagnorisis — The hero's moment of recognition
  • Catharsis — Emotional purging in the audience
  • Catastrophe — The final devastating outcome
Structure

Freytag's Pyramid

Gustav Freytag (1863) identified a five-act structure common to classical tragedies — a dramatic arc that rises to a climax, then plunges toward catastrophe.

Structure × Macbeth

The Pyramid in Macbeth

I · Exposition

Scotland is at war. Macbeth is a brave, respected general. The three witches prophesy he will become king, planting the seed of ambition.

II · Rising Action

Spurred by Lady Macbeth, he murders King Duncan and seizes the throne. Paranoia grows; he orders the killing of Banquo.

III · Climax

Banquo's ghost haunts the banquet. Macbeth revisits the witches and receives deceptive new prophecies, deepening his false sense of invincibility.

IV · Falling Action

Lady Macbeth unravels into madness. Malcolm raises an army in England. Scotland's lords abandon Macbeth. His tyranny isolates him completely.

V · Catastrophe

Lady Macbeth dies. Birnam Wood "moves" to Dunsinane. Macduff — not "of woman born" — kills Macbeth. Malcolm restores order as king.

Character Arc

Macbeth's Tragic Arc

Act I
Noble Warrior
"Brave Macbeth — well he deserves that name." He is loyal, valiant, and honoured by King Duncan.
Act I — The Prophecy
Seeds of Ambition
The witches' words ignite a hidden desire. He begins to contemplate murder: "If chance will have me king, why, chance may crown me."
Act II — The Murder
Point of No Return
He murders Duncan in his sleep. Immediate guilt shatters him: "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood clean from my hand?"
Act III
Tyrant King
Orders Banquo's assassination. Haunted by his ghost at the feast — paranoia consumes him.
Act IV
Deepening Darkness
Massacres Macduff's family. Seeks the witches again. His moral compass is completely destroyed.
Act V — Lady Macbeth's Death
Nihilistic Despair
"Life's but a walking shadow, a poor player that struts and frets his hour upon the stage, and then is heard no more."
Act V — Final Battle
Defiant Fall
Realises the prophecies were tricks. Fights Macduff knowing he will die: "Yet I will try the last." A final flash of his former courage.
Hamartia

What Makes Macbeth a Tragic Hero?

The Tragic Hero Checklist

  • Noble stature — Thane of Glamis, then Cawdor; a celebrated war hero respected by the king
  • Hamartia (fatal flaw) — Vaulting ambition, unchecked by moral restraint
  • Free choice — He chooses murder; no one forces the dagger into his hand
  • Punishment exceeds the crime — Total self-destruction, loss of sanity, love, honour, and life
  • Self-awareness — He knows what he does is wrong even before he does it
"I have no spur to prick the sides of my intent, but only vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself…" — Macbeth, Act I Scene 7

Why it matters

Macbeth is not a villain — he is a good man corrupted. His awareness of his own evil makes him tragic, not merely wicked. We watch someone with moral clarity choose darkness anyway.

Hamartia Deep Dive

Ambition: The Dagger of the Mind

Macbeth's hamartia — his fatal flaw — is not merely ambition, but ambition unmoored from morality. Shakespeare shows this through a careful arc:

1. Temptation

The witches do not command Macbeth — they simply reveal a possibility. His ambition already exists within him; the prophecy merely gives it voice.

2. Internal Battle

"Is this a dagger which I see before me?" — Macbeth hallucinates before the murder, showing the war between conscience and desire.

3. Moral Collapse

Each murder comes easier. From agonising over Duncan to casually ordering the slaughter of Macduff's family — ambition devours his humanity step by step.

Lady Macbeth as Catalyst

She doesn't create his ambition — she removes his hesitation. She questions his manhood, his courage, and his love. Without her push, the flaw might have remained dormant.

"Look like th' innocent flower, but be the serpent under't." — Lady Macbeth, Act I Scene 5

The Tragic Irony

Macbeth gains everything the witches promised — kingship, power, invincibility — yet loses everything that made life worth living: his honour, his wife, his peace of mind, his friends, and finally his life.

"I have lived long enough. My way of life is fall'n into the sere, the yellow leaf." — Macbeth, Act V Scene 3
Ancient Greece

The Purpose of Tragedy

For the Greeks, tragedy wasn't entertainment — it was a civic and spiritual practice performed at the Festival of Dionysus, designed to confront humanity's deepest fears.

Catharsis

Aristotle argued tragedy purges the audience of pity and fear — a collective emotional cleansing. Watching Macbeth's fall, we process our own darkest impulses safely.

Moral Warning

Tragedy shows the consequences of hubris — overstepping human limits. Macbeth's ambition mirrors the Greek heroes who defied the gods and paid the price.

Civic Reflection

Greek tragedies were performed for the entire polis (city-state) — they asked: What kind of leaders do we want? What happens when power is unchecked?

Understanding Fate

Are we masters of our destiny, or puppets of fate? Macbeth's witches echo the Greek oracles — the prophecy is fulfilled because Macbeth acts on it.

Shared Humanity

We see ourselves in the tragic hero. Macbeth is not alien to us — his ambition, guilt, and fear are universal. Tragedy reminds us: this could have been any of us.

Restoring Order

Tragedy ends with justice — Malcolm takes the throne, order returns. The Greeks believed suffering must lead to renewal. Destruction precedes restoration.

"Fair is foul, and foul is fair"

The tragedy of Macbeth endures because it holds a mirror to the darkness within us all — the ambition we dare not name, the lines we believe we'd never cross, and the terrible speed at which a good person can fall.
